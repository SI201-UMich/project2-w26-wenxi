Question 1: 
 
I believe the current system provides inadequate accountability for short-term rentals in San Francisco. 
The system is reactive rather than proactive as it only investigates listings after a complaint is filed, 
which means many unlicensed hosts can operate for long periods without consequence. 
This places the burden of enforcement on third parties like independent investigators or concerned residents rather 
than on the government or Airbnb itself. The SWE Code of Ethics emphasizes that software engineers should act in the public interest, 
which I think applies here to Airbnb as a platform. Airbnb could do more by automatically verifying policy numbers before allowing a 
listing to go live, rather than simply displaying whatever the host enters in a text field. A more proactive approach would be for the city 
to require Airbnb to cross-reference all listed policy numbers against the city's official registration database in real time, flagging or 
removing listings that cannot be verified. Without this kind of systemic enforcement, the current complaint-based model will continue to 
leave many violations unaddressed.
 
Question 2:
 
One important factor in the legality of web scraping is whether the data being accessed is truly public and whether the scraping 
causes harm to the platform or its users. The Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA) has historically been interpreted broadly, meaning 
that violating a website's Terms of Service could potentially be considered unauthorized access — even when the data itself is 
publicly visible to any browser. This is a significant concern because it creates legal uncertainty for researchers, journalists, 
and investigators who use scraping as a tool for accountability. I think the key distinction that courts and lawmakers should focus 
on is intent and impact: scraping publicly available data for research or public interest purposes is fundamentally different from 
scraping to undermine a platform's business or to harvest personal data maliciously. The law should reflect this difference more clearly
 rather than treating all scraping under the same broad umbrella.
 
Question 3: 
 
First, I think it is important to consider whether the scraping respects the privacy and reasonable expectations of the individuals 
whose data is being collected. Even if data is technically public, people who post information online often do so in a specific context. 
For example, a host listing their property on Airbnb does not necessarily expect that data to be aggregated, analyzed, and cross-referenced with
 government records. Before scraping, one should ask whether the people whose data is involved would reasonably consent to this use if asked. 
 Second, the purpose and potential impact of the scraping should be carefully evaluated. Scraping for public accountability, such as identifying 
 hosts who violate housing regulations, serves a clear social good. But the same technique used to build a mass surveillance database or to target 
 individuals — as seen with Clearview AI — can cause serious harm. A useful guideline is to ask whether the scraping produces a net benefit to 
 the public and whether that benefit outweighs the privacy costs to individuals. These two guidelines together — contextual privacy and public benefit — 
 can help determine whether a scraping project is ethically justifiable.
